Part A · History of the Atom
Atomic Theory Timeline
Our understanding of the atom has evolved dramatically over 2,500 years. Each scientist built on — and sometimes overturned — the models that came before.
💡 Key Idea

Science is never truly "finished." Atomic models have been revised multiple times as new experimental evidence emerged. This is the nature of scientific thinking!

~400 BCE
Democritus (Ancient Greece)
ATOMOS MODEL
Proposed that all matter is made of tiny, indivisible particles he called atomos (meaning "uncuttable"). This was pure philosophy — no experiments — but was remarkably insightful. However, Aristotle's opposing idea (matter was continuous) dominated for 2,000 years.
1803
John Dalton
SOLID SPHERE MODEL (Billiard Ball)
Used experimental data to propose that all matter is made of atoms; atoms of the same element are identical; atoms combine in whole-number ratios to form compounds. First scientific atomic theory. Key idea: atoms are indivisible, solid spheres.
1897
J.J. Thomson
PLUM PUDDING MODEL
Discovered the electron using cathode ray tubes. Proposed atoms are a diffuse ball of positive charge with electrons embedded throughout — like raisins in a pudding. First evidence that atoms have internal structure and are NOT indivisible.
1911
Ernest Rutherford
NUCLEAR MODEL
Gold foil experiment: fired alpha particles at gold foil — most passed through, but some bounced back. Concluded atoms are mostly empty space with a tiny, dense, positively-charged nucleus at the center. Electrons orbit the nucleus in empty space.
1913
Niels Bohr
PLANETARY / BOHR MODEL
Proposed electrons travel in specific, fixed circular orbits (energy levels) around the nucleus. Electrons can jump between orbits by absorbing or releasing energy. Explained the hydrogen emission spectrum. This is the model still taught in most middle/high school classes.
1926+
Schrödinger, Heisenberg & others
QUANTUM MECHANICAL MODEL (Current Model)
Electrons do NOT travel in fixed paths. Instead, they exist in probability clouds called orbitals — regions of space where an electron is most likely to be found. This is the modern, accepted model of the atom.
🔬 Rutherford's Gold Foil Experiment

Expected result (Thomson model): alpha particles should pass through with minimal deflection.
Actual result: Most passed through, but ~1 in 8,000 bounced back almost directly! Rutherford said this was like firing artillery shells at tissue paper and having them bounce back. This proved the nucleus exists.

Part A · History of the Atom
Structure of the Atom
Every atom has a nucleus surrounded by electrons. The number of protons defines what element it is.
Subatomic Particles
  • Protons (p⁺) — Positive charge, found in the nucleus. The number of protons = atomic number → identifies the element.
  • Neutrons (n⁰) — No charge, found in the nucleus. Protons + Neutrons = mass number.
  • Electrons (e⁻) — Negative charge, found in energy levels (shells) outside the nucleus. In a neutral atom: # electrons = # protons.
⚖️ Isotopes

Atoms of the same element that have different numbers of neutrons. Same atomic number, different mass number.

Example: Carbon-12 has 6 neutrons; Carbon-14 has 8 neutrons. Both are carbon!

⚡ Ions

Atoms that have gained or lost electrons, giving them a positive or negative charge.

Lost e⁻ → positive ion (cation). Gained e⁻ → negative ion (anion).

📝 Reading the Periodic Table

For any element on the periodic table:
• Top number = Atomic Number (# of protons)
• Letter(s) = Chemical symbol
• Bottom number = Atomic Mass (protons + average neutrons)
• # of electrons = Same as atomic number (in neutral atom)

🗺️ How the Table is Organized
  • Periods (rows) — Elements in the same row have the same number of electron energy levels.
  • Groups/Families (columns) — Elements in the same column have similar chemical properties because they have the same number of valence electrons.
  • Atomic number increases left to right, top to bottom.
Part B · Periodic Table
Trends & Properties
The periodic table is a powerful predictive tool. Knowing where an element sits tells you a lot about how it will behave.
🔵 Metals
  • Shiny, lustrous appearance
  • Good conductors of heat and electricity
  • Malleable and ductile
  • Tend to lose electrons → form positive ions
  • Examples: Iron (Fe), Copper (Cu), Gold (Au), Sodium (Na)
🟢 Nonmetals
  • Dull appearance (except iodine)
  • Poor conductors (insulators)
  • Brittle in solid form
  • Tend to gain electrons → form negative ions
  • Examples: Oxygen (O), Carbon (C), Sulfur (S), Nitrogen (N)
🟡 Metalloids (Semimetals)
  • Properties of BOTH metals and nonmetals
  • Semi-conductors — key in electronics!
  • Located on the "staircase" between metals/nonmetals
  • Examples: Silicon (Si), Boron (B), Arsenic (As)
🟣 Noble Gases
  • Last column (Group 18)
  • Full outer electron shells → extremely unreactive
  • All gases at room temperature
  • Examples: Helium (He), Neon (Ne), Argon (Ar)
⚠️ Special Families to Know
  • Alkali Metals (Group 1) — Very reactive metals; 1 valence electron; react violently with water.
  • Alkaline Earth Metals (Group 2) — Reactive metals; 2 valence electrons; less reactive than Group 1.
  • Halogens (Group 17) — Very reactive nonmetals; 7 valence electrons; need just 1 more to fill their shell.
  • Transition Metals (Groups 3–12) — Less reactive, found in the middle block; include iron, copper, gold, silver.
📈 Periodic Trends (SOL-Level)
  • Moving across a period (→): Atomic radius decreases; reactivity of metals decreases; reactivity of nonmetals increases.
  • Moving down a group (↓): Atomic radius increases; metals become more reactive; halogens become less reactive.

Part C · Kinetic Molecular Theory
States of Matter
Matter exists in different states depending on the energy of its particles. Click each state to learn more.
🧊
SOLID
Definite shape & volume. Particles vibrate in fixed positions.
💧
LIQUID
Definite volume, no definite shape. Particles flow past each other.
☁️
GAS
No definite shape or volume. Particles move rapidly and spread out.
⚡
PLASMA
Superheated, ionized gas. The most common state in the universe.
🔥 Phase Changes
  • Melting: solid → liquid (absorbs energy)
  • Freezing: liquid → solid (releases energy)
  • Evaporation/Boiling: liquid → gas (absorbs energy)
  • Condensation: gas → liquid (releases energy)
  • Sublimation: solid → gas (absorbs energy)
  • Deposition: gas → solid (releases energy)
📏 Physical vs. Chemical Changes
  • Physical change: Changes form or state, but NOT chemical composition. (Ice melting, tearing paper)
  • Chemical change: Produces new substances with different properties. (Burning, rusting, cooking)
  • Phase changes are physical changes — the substance is still the same!
Part C · Virtual Lab
KMT Simulation Lab
The Kinetic Molecular Theory states that all matter is made of particles in constant motion. Use the controls below to see how particles behave in different states.
Particles vibrate in fixed positions — low energy
❄️ Solid — KMT
  • Particles are closely packed in a regular, ordered pattern
  • Particles vibrate in place — do NOT move freely
  • Very low kinetic energy
  • Strong attractive forces hold shape
💧 Liquid — KMT
  • Particles are close but can move past each other
  • Medium kinetic energy
  • Weaker forces than solid — can flow
  • Volume is constant, but shape takes the container
☁️ Gas — KMT
  • Particles are far apart, moving rapidly in random directions
  • Very high kinetic energy
  • Almost no attractive forces between particles
  • Fills any container completely
🌡️ Temperature & Kinetic Energy Connection

Temperature is a measure of the average kinetic energy of particles. Higher temperature = faster-moving particles = more kinetic energy. This is why heating a solid turns it into a liquid, and heating a liquid turns it into a gas — energy overcomes the forces holding particles together.
