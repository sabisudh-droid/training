8th Grade Physical Science
🚀Introduction to Energy Conservation

Explore how energy is stored, transferred, and transformed — and why it's never truly "lost."

SOL PS.5 — Key Ideas
  • a) Energy can be stored in different ways.
  • b) Energy is transferred and transformed.
  • c) Energy can be transformed to meet societal needs.

What Is Energy?

Energy is the ability to do work or cause change. It exists in many forms and can move between objects and systems — but it cannot be created or destroyed.

Law of Conservation of Energy

Energy cannot be created or destroyed — it can only be transferred or transformed from one form to another. The total amount of energy in a closed system always remains constant.

Why Does Energy Matter?

Everything you do uses energy — from walking to school to charging your phone. Understanding energy helps scientists and engineers:

  • Design more efficient machines and vehicles
  • Develop renewable energy sources to reduce pollution
  • Understand natural processes like weather and ecosystems
  • Solve global challenges like climate change

The Two Big Categories

All forms of energy fall into two major categories:

  • Kinetic Energy — energy of motion. Any object that is moving has kinetic energy. The faster it moves and the more mass it has, the greater its kinetic energy.
  • Potential Energy — energy that is stored due to position or condition. A stretched spring, a book on a shelf, and food all contain potential energy.
Key Insight

Energy constantly switches between kinetic and potential — like a swinging pendulum or a roller coaster!

Potential energy is stored energy that has the potential to be converted into kinetic energy. Below are the major types you'll need to know:

⬆️

🧪

Stored in bonds — food, batteries, fossil fuels, TNT
🌡️
Thermal
Heat energy — related to the movement of particles
⚛️
Nuclear
Stored in atomic nuclei — released in fission/fusion reactions
💡
Radiant (Light)
Electromagnetic radiation — sunlight, microwaves, X-rays

Elastic Potential Energy

A stretched or compressed elastic material (spring, rubber band, bow) stores elastic potential energy. When released, this energy converts to kinetic energy. The more you stretch or compress, the more energy is stored.

Real World Example

When you pull back a slingshot, you store elastic potential energy in the stretched rubber. When released, that energy converts to kinetic energy in the projectile — this is energy transformation in action!

⚡Energy Transfer & Transformation

Key Idea B: Energy moves between objects (transfer) and changes form (transformation).

Energy Transfer vs. Transformation

Energy Transfer — Energy moves from one object or location to another without changing form.

Energy Transformation — Energy changes from one form to another. This is also called energy conversion.

Key Distinction

When you push a box across the floor, you transfer kinetic energy from your muscles to the box. When a battery powers a light bulb, chemical energy is transformed into electrical, then light and heat energy.

Methods of Heat Transfer

Thermal energy (heat) transfers in three main ways:

  • Conduction — Direct contact between particles. Heat travels through solids. (Example: touching a hot pan)
  • Convection — Heat carried by moving fluids (liquids or gases). (Example: boiling water, weather patterns)
  • Radiation — Energy travels as electromagnetic waves — no medium needed. (Example: sunlight warming Earth)

Energy Is Always Conserved

During every transfer and transformation, the total energy remains constant. However, some energy is often transformed into forms that are less useful — usually heat (thermal energy) due to friction.

Example: Light Bulb

A traditional incandescent bulb converts electrical energy to light — but about 90% becomes wasted heat. Only ~10% is visible light. An LED bulb converts ~40% to light, wasting far less. Energy is conserved either way — just transformed differently.

🌍Energy & Society

Key Idea C: How energy transformations are engineered to meet human needs — and the trade-offs involved.

Energy for Human Civilization

Every technology humans use depends on energy transformations. Engineers design systems to maximize useful energy output while minimizing waste. The choice of energy source has major environmental, economic, and social consequences.

☀️
Solar Power
Photovoltaic cells transform radiant (light) energy → electrical energy. Clean, renewable, no emissions. Dependent on sunlight availability.
💨
Wind Energy
Wind turbines transform kinetic energy (moving air) → mechanical → electrical. Renewable, but wind is intermittent and turbines affect wildlife.
💧
Hydroelectric Power
Gravitational PE (water held behind a dam) → kinetic energy → electrical. Reliable and renewable, but dams alter river ecosystems.
🌿
Biomass / Biofuels
Chemical energy stored in organic material (wood, crops, waste) → thermal → mechanical → electrical. Carbon-neutral if sustainably managed.
⚛️
Nuclear Power
Nuclear PE → thermal (via fission) → mechanical → electrical. Extremely energy-dense with no CO₂ emissions, but produces radioactive waste.
🔋
Energy Efficiency
Reducing wasted energy in transformations. LED bulbs, hybrid vehicles, and insulation are examples. Less waste = lower cost and less pollution.

Trade-Offs in Energy Choices

No energy source is perfect. Scientists, engineers, and citizens must evaluate trade-offs between cost, reliability, environmental impact, land use, safety, and energy density. Virginia's energy future involves transitioning to more renewable sources while managing grid reliability.

SOL Connection

In Virginia, the 2020 Clean Economy Act set goals to achieve 100% carbon-free electricity by 2050. This requires massive energy transformation: shifting from chemical energy (coal, natural gas) to radiant and kinetic energy sources (solar, wind) — all grounded in the same physics you're learning here.
