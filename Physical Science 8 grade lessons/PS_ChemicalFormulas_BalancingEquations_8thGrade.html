Module 1 · Introduction
Reading & Writing the Language of Chemistry
Chemical formulas are the shorthand language chemists use to describe matter. Balanced equations show that matter is never created or destroyed — only rearranged. This lesson is aligned to Virginia SOL PS.5c.

Every substance in the universe can be described by a chemical formula — a combination of element symbols and subscript numbers that tells you exactly which atoms are present and how many.

When atoms rearrange to form new substances, we write a chemical equation. A balanced equation obeys the Law of Conservation of Mass — the same number of each type of atom must appear on both sides.

Slide 1 of 8
🔤 What Is a Chemical Formula?

A chemical formula uses element symbols and numbers to show the composition of a substance.

  • Symbols represent elements (e.g., H = Hydrogen, O = Oxygen, Na = Sodium)
  • Subscripts (small numbers after symbols) show how many atoms of that element are in one unit
  • If no subscript is written, it means 1 atom
💡 Quick Examples
NaCl — 1 sodium + 1 chlorine (table salt)
H₂O — 2 hydrogen + 1 oxygen (water)
CO₂ — 1 carbon + 2 oxygen (carbon dioxide)
C₆H₁₂O₆ — 6 carbon + 12 hydrogen + 6 oxygen (glucose)
Slide 2 of 8
🔢 Subscripts vs. Coefficients — Don't Mix Them Up!

Two kinds of numbers appear in chemical equations. Knowing the difference is essential for balancing.

⚠️ Critical Rule
Never change a subscript to balance an equation — that would change the identity of the substance! You may only add or change coefficients (the large numbers in front of formulas).
Slide 3 of 8
⚖️ Law of Conservation of Mass

The Law of Conservation of Mass states: matter is neither created nor destroyed in a chemical reaction. The total mass of the reactants always equals the total mass of the products.

This means every atom present before a reaction must still be present after — just arranged differently into new substances.

🔬 Antoine Lavoisier (1789)
The Law of Conservation of Mass was established by French chemist Antoine Lavoisier, who showed through careful experiments that the total mass before and after a chemical reaction is always equal — even if the substances look completely different.
Slide 4 of 8
📝 Anatomy of a Chemical Equation

A chemical equation has a specific structure. Let's break down a real example — the formation of water:

  • Reactants — substances that enter the reaction (left of arrow)
  • Products — substances produced (right of arrow)
  • Coefficients — numbers in front of formulas showing how many molecules
  • The → (yield arrow) means "produces" or "reacts to form"
🔢 Atom Count in 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O
Reactants: 2×2=4 H + 2×1=2 O  |  Products: 2×2=4 H + 2×1=2 O ✓ Balanced!
Slide 5 of 8
⚖️ How to Balance an Equation — Step by Step

Balancing equations is a systematic process. Follow these steps every time:

Step 1: Write the unbalanced equation with correct formulas.

Step 2: Count atoms of each element on both sides.

Step 3: Add coefficients to make counts equal. Start with the most complex molecule.

Step 4: Re-count all atoms to verify balance.

Step 5: Make sure all coefficients are the smallest possible whole numbers.

Worked Example: Burning methane (CH₄ + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O)

Unbalanced: CH4 + O2 → CO2 + H2O
Count C: left=1, right=1 ✓
Count H: left=4, right=2 ✗ → add 2 in front of H₂O
Count O: left=2, right=2+2=4 ✗ → add 2 in front of O₂
Balanced: CH4 + 2O2 → CO2 + 2H2O ✓
C: 1=1 ✓   H: 4=4 ✓   O: 4=4 ✓

🔥 Balancing Example: Synthesis of Water

Let's walk through balancing the formation of water from hydrogen and oxygen gases — a classic synthesis reaction.

✅ Verify the Final Answer: 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O
H: (2×2)=4 on left  |  (2×2)=4 on right ✓
O: (1×2)=2 on left  |  (2×1)=2 on right ✓
Slide 7 of 8
🧪 More Balancing Examples

Let's practice with three more common reactions you'll see on the SOL test:

Example 1 — Iron rusting (Combination reaction):

Unbalanced: Fe + O2 → Fe2O3
Balanced: 4Fe + 3O2 → 2Fe2O3
Fe: 4=4 ✓   O: 6=6 ✓

Example 2 — Decomposition of water:

Unbalanced: H2O → H2 + O2
Balanced: 2H2O → 2H2 + O2
H: 4=4 ✓   O: 2=2 ✓

Example 3 — Sodium + Chlorine (Synthesis):

Unbalanced: Na + Cl2 → NaCl
Balanced: 2Na + Cl2 → 2NaCl
Na: 2=2 ✓   Cl: 2=2 ✓

📚 Key Takeaways — VA SOL PS.5c

1. Chemical formulas use element symbols and subscripts to show the composition of substances (e.g., H₂O = 2 H atoms + 1 O atom).

2. Subscripts show atoms per molecule and cannot be changed when balancing. Only coefficients (numbers in front) may be adjusted.

3. The Law of Conservation of Mass requires that the total number of atoms of each element is the same on both sides of a balanced equation.

4. Balancing steps: write → count → add coefficients → verify → simplify to lowest whole numbers.

5. Reaction types — synthesis (A+B→AB), decomposition (AB→A+B), single/double replacement, combustion — each has a recognizable pattern.
