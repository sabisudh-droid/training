Module 1 · Introduction
What Holds Matter Together?
In this lesson, you'll explore how atoms form chemical bonds, the types of bonds that exist, and why bonding determines the properties of substances — all aligned to Virginia SOL PS.4c.

Everything around you — the desk you sit at, the water you drink, the air you breathe — is made of atoms joined together by chemical bonds. A chemical bond is a force of attraction that holds two atoms together.

Atoms bond because doing so makes them more stable. Most atoms seek a full outer electron shell — usually 8 valence electrons (the octet rule). They achieve this by sharing, gaining, or losing electrons.

⚡
Ionic Bond
Transfer of electrons between metal and nonmetal
🔗
Covalent Bond
Sharing electrons between two nonmetals
🪙
Metallic Bond
Sea of electrons shared across metal atoms
🎯 Learning Objectives — VA SOL PS.4c
After completing this lesson, you will be able to:
• Identify and describe ionic, covalent, and metallic bonds
• Explain how valence electrons determine bond type
• Distinguish properties of ionic vs. covalent compounds
• Interpret simple chemical formulas and bond diagrams

⚛️ Valence Electrons — The Key to Bonding

Atoms have electrons arranged in energy levels called electron shells. The outermost shell holds the valence electrons — these are the only electrons involved in bonding.

  • Sodium (Na) has 1 valence electron → wants to lose it
  • Chlorine (Cl) has 7 valence electrons → wants to gain 1
  • Oxygen (O) has 6 valence electrons → wants to gain 2

The number of valence electrons determines how an atom will bond. You can find this from the group number on the Periodic Table!

🔑 Octet Rule
Atoms are most stable when they have 8 valence electrons (like noble gases). Bonding is atoms' way of achieving this stability.
Slide 2 of 10
⚡ Ionic Bonds — Electron Transfer

An ionic bond forms when one atom transfers one or more electrons to another atom, creating oppositely charged ions that attract each other.

  • Cations (+): atoms that LOSE electrons → usually metals
  • Anions (−): atoms that GAIN electrons → usually nonmetals
🔬 Properties of Ionic Compounds
• Form rigid crystal (lattice) structures
• Very high melting & boiling points
• Conduct electricity when dissolved in water
• Often soluble in water — examples: NaCl, MgO, CaCl₂

➡ The next two slides show step-by-step diagrams of how ionic bonds form in NaCl and MgO.

Slide 3 of 10
⚡ Ionic Bond Diagram: Formation of NaCl (Table Salt)
📌 Step-by-Step Summary
Step 1: Na has 1 valence electron (Group 1) — it is unstable.
Step 2: Cl has 7 valence electrons (Group 17) — needs 1 more to fill its shell.
Step 3: Na transfers its 1 valence electron to Cl.
Step 4: Na becomes Na⁺ (positive cation); Cl becomes Cl⁻ (negative anion).
Step 5: Opposite charges attract → ionic bond forms → NaCl crystal lattice (table salt)!
Slide 4 of 10
⚡ Ionic Bond: NaCl Crystal Lattice + Ion Recap

After electron transfer, Na⁺ and Cl⁻ ions arrange themselves in a repeating 3-D pattern called a crystal lattice. Each Na⁺ is surrounded by 6 Cl⁻ ions and vice versa.

💡 Why are ionic compounds brittle?
If the lattice is struck, rows shift — same charges align → repel each other → the crystal shatters! This explains why table salt breaks into flat pieces rather than bending.
Slide 5 of 10
🔗 Covalent Bonds — Electron Sharing

A covalent bond forms when two atoms share one or more pairs of electrons. This usually happens between two nonmetals.

  • Single bond: 1 shared pair (e.g., Cl–Cl, H–H)
  • Double bond: 2 shared pairs (e.g., O=O in O₂)
  • Triple bond: 3 shared pairs (e.g., N≡N)

Polar vs. Nonpolar Covalent Bonds:

  • Nonpolar covalent: electrons shared equally between identical atoms (Cl₂, O₂, H₂)
  • Polar covalent: electrons shared unequally — more electronegative atom pulls harder (H₂O)
🔬 Properties of Covalent Compounds
• Form discrete molecules (not lattices)
• Lower melting/boiling points than ionic compounds
• Usually do NOT conduct electricity
• Examples: H₂O, CO₂, Cl₂, O₂, sugar

➡ The next slides show diagrams of Cl₂ (single covalent), O₂ (double covalent), and H₂O (polar covalent).

Slide 6 of 10
🔗 Covalent Bond Diagram: Cl₂ (Chlorine Molecule) — Single Bond
📌 Step-by-Step: Cl₂ Formation
Step 1: Each Cl atom has 7 valence electrons — needs 1 more to reach 8 (octet).
Step 2: Each Cl contributes 1 electron to a shared pair between them.
Step 3: The shared pair "belongs" to both atoms — both now effectively have 8 valence electrons.
Result: Cl₂ — a nonpolar covalent single bond (electrons shared equally, same element).
Slide 7 of 10
🔗 Covalent Bond Diagram: O₂ (Oxygen Molecule) — Double Bond
📌 Step-by-Step: O₂ Formation
Step 1: Each O atom has 6 valence electrons — needs 2 more to complete the octet.
Step 2: Each O contributes 2 electrons to form 2 shared pairs between them.
Step 3: Both O atoms now effectively have 8 valence electrons (2 lone pairs + 2 shared pairs × 2).
Result: O₂ — a nonpolar covalent double bond. Double bonds are shorter and stronger than single bonds!
Slide 8 of 10
🔗 Covalent Bond Diagram: H₂O (Water) — Polar Covalent
📌 Why is H₂O polar (not nonpolar)?
Step 1: O has 6 valence electrons; each H has 1. O needs 2 more; each H needs 1 more.
Step 2: O shares 1 electron with each H → 2 single covalent bonds formed.
Step 3: Oxygen is more electronegative — it pulls shared electrons closer to itself.
Step 4: O gains a partial negative charge (δ⁻); each H gains partial positive (δ⁺).
Result: H₂O is a polar covalent molecule with a bent shape — this is why water is such a powerful solvent!
Slide 9 of 10
🪙 Metallic Bonds — The Electron Sea

Metallic bonds occur in pure metals and alloys. Metal atoms release their valence electrons into a shared "sea of electrons" that flows freely throughout the material.

  • Positive metal ions sit in a fixed lattice
  • Mobile electrons flow freely through the metal
  • The attraction between positive ions and the electron sea holds everything together
🔬 Properties of Metals (explained by metallic bonding)
• Conductivity: free electrons carry electric current and heat
• Malleability: layers of ions can slide — electrons adjust — doesn't shatter
• Ductility: can be drawn into wires
• Luster: free electrons interact with light → shiny surface
• High melting points (strong attraction between ions and electrons)

Examples: copper wire (Cu), aluminum foil (Al), iron (Fe), gold jewelry (Au)

📚 Key Takeaways — VA SOL PS.4c

1. Chemical bonds hold atoms together through interactions of valence electrons.

2. Ionic bonds — electron transfer between metal + nonmetal → creates ions, forms crystals, conducts electricity when dissolved.

3. Covalent bonds — electron sharing between nonmetals → forms molecules, generally lower melting points, usually don't conduct electricity.

4. Metallic bonds — "sea of electrons" in metals → explains conductivity, malleability, and luster.

5. The Octet Rule drives bonding — atoms seek 8 valence electrons for stability.

6. Lewis dot diagrams model valence electrons and predict how atoms will bond.
