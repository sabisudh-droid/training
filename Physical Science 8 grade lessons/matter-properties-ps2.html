Introduction

Matter & Its Properties

Everything around you — the air you breathe, the desk you sit at, even you — is made of matter. In this lesson you'll explore how we identify matter by its properties and how matter changes in physical and chemical processes.

📋 VIRGINIA SOL OBJECTIVES — PS.2

◆Pure substances can be identified based on their chemical and physical properties.
◆Pure substances can undergo physical changes that may result in a change of properties.
◆Pure substances can undergo chemical changes that may result in a change of properties.
◆Matter is conserved in both physical and chemical processes.
⚗️

Color, density, boiling point — properties you can observe without changing the substance's identity.

🔥

Chemical Properties

⚖️

Conservation of Matter

MODULE 01
What Is Matter?
The foundation of physical science

📌 DEFINITION

Matter is anything that has mass and takes up space (volume). All matter is made of tiny particles called atoms.

States of Matter

🧊

Solid

Definite shape and volume. Particles vibrate in fixed positions. Example: ice, rock, metal.

💧

Liquid

Definite volume, no definite shape. Particles move past each other. Example: water, mercury, juice.

💨

Gas

No definite shape or volume. Particles move quickly and spread out. Example: oxygen, steam.

⚡

Plasma

High-energy ionized gas. Found in the sun, lightning, and neon lights. Very high temperature.

🔬 PURE SUBSTANCES vs. MIXTURES

  • Element – contains only one type of atom (e.g., gold Au, oxygen O₂)
  • Compound – two or more elements chemically bonded (e.g., water H₂O, table salt NaCl)
  • Mixture – substances combined but NOT chemically bonded; can be separated physically

💡 KEY IDEA

Only pure substances (elements and compounds) have specific, consistent physical and chemical properties. Mixtures do not have fixed properties — they depend on what's in the mixture.

MODULE 02
Physical Properties
Characteristics you can observe without changing the substance's identity

📌 DEFINITION

A physical property is a characteristic of matter that can be observed or measured without changing the chemical identity of the substance.

Types of Physical Properties

Property Description Example Type
Mass Amount of matter in an object A rock has more mass than a feather Extensive
Volume Amount of space matter occupies A basketball has more volume than a golf ball Extensive
Density Mass per unit volume (D = m/v) Iron sinks; wood floats in water Intensive
Color Wavelength of light reflected Copper is reddish-brown Intensive
Melting Point Temperature at which solid becomes liquid Ice melts at 0°C / 32°F Intensive
Boiling Point Temperature at which liquid becomes gas Water boils at 100°C / 212°F Intensive
Solubility Ability to dissolve in a solvent Salt dissolves in water; oil does not Intensive
Conductivity Ability to conduct heat or electricity Copper wire conducts electricity Intensive

📐 EXTENSIVE PROPERTIES

Depend on the amount of matter present. If you cut a sample in half, the property changes.
Examples: mass, volume, length

🔷 INTENSIVE PROPERTIES

Do NOT depend on the amount of matter. The same no matter how large or small the sample.
Examples: density, boiling point, color

✅ WHY IT MATTERS FOR PS.2

Intensive physical properties like density, melting point, and boiling point are used to identify pure substances — because they are the same for every pure sample of that substance.

MODULE 03
Chemical Properties
How a substance changes into a different substance

📌 DEFINITION

A chemical property describes a substance's ability to change into a different substance. Observing a chemical property means a chemical reaction has occurred and the original substance no longer exists in its original form.

🔥

Flammability

The ability to burn in the presence of oxygen. Wood is flammable; water is not.

🦀

Reactivity

The ability to react with other substances. Sodium reacts violently with water.

🟤

Oxidation / Rusting

Iron reacts with oxygen to form rust (iron oxide). A new substance is formed.

🧪

Reactivity with Acid

Zinc reacts with hydrochloric acid to produce hydrogen gas — a new substance.

⚡

Toxicity

The ability of a substance to cause harm when it reacts with body chemistry.

💥

Explosiveness

The ability to rapidly release energy through a chemical reaction. Example: dynamite.

🔑 KEY COMPARISON

  • Physical property: You can observe it WITHOUT changing the substance. (You can see that iron is shiny.)
  • Chemical property: You can only observe it BY changing the substance. (You see that iron rusts — now it's a new substance.)
MODULE 04
Physical Changes
Changes in form — but NOT in chemical identity

📌 DEFINITION

A physical change alters the form or appearance of matter, but does not change its chemical composition. The same substance is still present after the change.

Examples of Physical Changes

🧊→💧

Change of State

Melting ice, freezing water, boiling water into steam. H₂O is still H₂O.

✂️

Cutting / Tearing

Cutting paper into pieces. The paper is smaller but still the same substance.

🔨

Bending / Crushing

Crushing a can, bending a wire. Shape changes; substance stays the same.

🍬

Dissolving

Sugar dissolving in water. The sugar is still sugar — it can be recovered by evaporation.

💡 CLUES THAT A CHANGE IS PHYSICAL

  • The substance looks different but is still the same material
  • The change can often be reversed (freeze water back to ice)
  • No new substance is formed
  • The chemical formula does not change

⚠️ PROPERTIES THAT CHANGE vs. STAY THE SAME

In a physical change, intensive properties (like boiling point and density) stay the same, but extensive properties (like mass and volume of the pieces) may appear to change because the substance is divided. The total mass is still conserved.

MODULE 05
Chemical Changes
Changes that produce one or more new substances

📌 DEFINITION

A chemical change (chemical reaction) produces one or more new substances with different properties than the original materials. The original substance no longer exists in its original form.

Signs of a Chemical Change

🫧

Gas Production

Bubbles of gas form that were not there before. Example: vinegar + baking soda → CO₂

🌡️

Temperature Change

Heat is absorbed or released. Burning wood releases heat and light.

🎨

Color Change

A new color appears, indicating a new substance. Iron turning reddish-brown (rust).

🧱

Precipitate Forms

A solid forms when two liquids are mixed. Example: mixing certain salt solutions.

💡

Light Produced

Light energy is emitted. Burning, glow sticks, and chemiluminescence.

👃

Odor Change

A new smell forms as new substances are produced. Cooking food, burning wood.

🔵 PHYSICAL CHANGE

  • Same substance remains
  • Often reversible
  • No new substance formed
  • Change of state, shape, size
  • Chemical formula unchanged
VS

🟣 CHEMICAL CHANGE

  • New substance(s) formed
  • Usually NOT reversible
  • Different properties result
  • New bonds formed/broken
  • Chemical formula changes
MODULE 06
Conservation of Matter
Matter is never created or destroyed

⚖️ LAW OF CONSERVATION OF MATTER

In any physical or chemical change, the total mass of the products equals the total mass of the reactants. Matter is neither created nor destroyed — it only changes form.

In Physical Changes

EXAMPLE: MELTING ICE

  • 200 g of ice melts → 200 g of liquid water
  • The H₂O molecules are the same — only arrangement changed
  • Mass before = mass after ✓

EXAMPLE: CUTTING A WIRE

  • A 10 g copper wire cut in half → two 5 g pieces
  • Total: 5 + 5 = 10 g — still conserved ✓

In Chemical Changes

EXAMPLE: BURNING WOOD

  • Wood + Oxygen → CO₂ + Water + Ash
  • Ash seems lighter — but gases escaped into air!
  • If ALL products captured: mass is conserved ✓

BALANCED EQUATION

2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O

4 H atoms + 2 O atoms → 4 H atoms + 2 O atoms ✓

📊 CONSERVATION IN EQUATIONS

  • In a balanced chemical equation, the number of atoms of each element is the same on both sides
  • This is the mathematical expression of conservation of matter
  • In physical changes, the chemical formula doesn't change at all — conservation is automatic
