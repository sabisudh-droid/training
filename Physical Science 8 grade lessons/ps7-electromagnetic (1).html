What Is Electromagnetic Radiation?

Module 1 of 7 · Introduction

Electromagnetic (EM) radiation is energy that travels through space as oscillating electric and magnetic fields. Unlike sound waves, EM waves do not require a medium — they can travel through the vacuum of space!

Key Idea: All electromagnetic radiation travels at the same speed in a vacuum — the speed of light: ~300,000,000 m/s (3 × 10⁸ m/s). This is called c.

🔋 TWO FIELDS IN ONE

EM radiation is made of two perpendicular waves oscillating together:

  • An electric field wave
  • A magnetic field wave
  • Both perpendicular to the direction of travel

🚀 NO MEDIUM NEEDED

EM waves are transverse waves that need no matter to travel through. That's why:

  • Sunlight reaches Earth through space
  • Radio signals travel to satellites
  • X-rays pass through the body

Wave Characteristics

Module 2 of 7 · How EM waves behave

📏

Wavelength

λ (lambda)

Distance from one crest to the next. Measured in meters (m), nanometers (nm), or other units.

🔁

Frequency

f (Hz)

Number of complete waves passing a point per second. Measured in Hertz (Hz).

⚡

Energy

E (Joules)

Amount of energy carried by the wave. Higher frequency = more energy per photon.

📐

Amplitude

A

Height of the wave from rest position to crest. Related to the intensity (brightness) of light.

🚀

Wave Speed

c = f × λ

All EM waves travel at c = 3×10⁸ m/s in a vacuum, regardless of frequency.

🔄

Period

T = 1/f

Time for one complete wave cycle to pass. Inverse of frequency.

⚖️ WAVELENGTH VS. FREQUENCY RELATIONSHIP

Inverse Relationship: As wavelength increases → frequency decreases. As wavelength decreases → frequency increases. Since all EM waves travel at the same speed (c), a shorter wavelength must "squeeze" more waves per second = higher frequency.

This also means: Higher frequency = more energy. Gamma rays have the highest frequency and most energy. Radio waves have the lowest frequency and least energy.

📖 WAVE BEHAVIORS

All EM radiation exhibits these wave behaviors:

  • Reflection — bouncing off surfaces
  • Refraction — bending when entering a new medium
  • Absorption — energy taken in by matter
  • Diffraction — bending around obstacles or through openings
  • Interference — waves combining to amplify or cancel
  • Transmission — passing through a material

The Electromagnetic Spectrum

Module 3 of 7 · All regions overview

Regions & Real-World Uses

Module 4 of 7 · Characteristics and applications

Each region of the EM spectrum has unique characteristics that make it useful for specific technologies. Explore each region below.

📻

Radio Waves

Longest wavelengths, lowest frequency and energy. Can travel great distances and pass through buildings.

Uses: Radio/TV broadcasting, MRI scanners, WiFi, cell phones
📡

Microwaves

Wavelengths 1 mm to 1 m. Can excite water molecules, producing heat. Penetrate clouds and rain.

Uses: Microwave ovens, radar, satellite communication, GPS
🔥

Infrared (IR)

Emitted by warm objects as heat. Wavelengths just longer than visible red light. We feel IR as warmth.

Uses: TV remotes, night vision, thermal imaging, heat lamps
👁️

Visible Light

The only region detectable by the human eye. Ranges from ~380 nm (violet) to ~700 nm (red).

Uses: Vision, photography, fiber optic communication, lasers
☀️

Ultraviolet (UV)

Higher energy than visible light. Causes sunburn, but also destroys bacteria. Produces fluorescence.

Uses: Sterilization, black lights, detecting counterfeit bills, tanning
🦴

X-Rays

High energy; can penetrate soft tissue but is partially blocked by dense materials like bone.

Uses: Medical imaging, airport security scanning, studying crystals
☢️

Gamma Rays

Highest frequency and energy. Produced by nuclear reactions and radioactive decay. Most penetrating.

Uses: Cancer treatment (radiation therapy), sterilizing food, studying universe

⚠️ IONIZING VS. NON-IONIZING RADIATION

Non-Ionizing (lower energy)

Radio, Microwave, Infrared, Visible — not enough energy to remove electrons from atoms. Generally safer for human exposure.

Ionizing (higher energy)

UV, X-ray, Gamma — enough energy to remove electrons from atoms, potentially damaging DNA. Requires safety precautions.

Visible Light & Wave Behavior

Module 5 of 7 · Our window into the spectrum

Visible light is the narrow band of EM radiation that human eyes can detect, spanning wavelengths from approximately 380 nm (violet) to 700 nm (red). The colors we see are determined by the wavelength of the light entering our eyes.

Violet 380nmBlueGreenYellowOrangeRed 700nm

〰️ WAVE BEHAVIORS OF VISIBLE LIGHT

Visible light demonstrates all wave behaviors. These behaviors explain many everyday phenomena:

BehaviorExample in Visible Light
ReflectionSeeing your face in a mirror; moonlight (reflected sunlight)
RefractionRainbow formation; straw appearing bent in water; lenses
AbsorptionBlack asphalt heating up; colored objects absorbing certain wavelengths
DiffractionLight spreading around edges; iridescent colors on a CD surface
InterferenceSoap bubble colors; oil slick rainbow patterns
TransmissionLight passing through glass, water, or a window

🔴 COLOR & WAVELENGTH

  • Violet — 380–450 nm (highest energy)
  • Blue — 450–495 nm
  • Green — 495–570 nm
  • Yellow — 570–590 nm
  • Orange — 590–625 nm
  • Red — 625–700 nm (lowest energy)

🌤️ WHY IS THE SKY BLUE?

Sunlight contains all colors. When it enters Earth's atmosphere, gas molecules scatter blue light (shorter wavelength) much more than red light. This scattered blue light comes from all directions, making the sky appear blue.
