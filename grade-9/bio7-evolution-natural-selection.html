What is Evolution?
Change in populations over time
Definition
Evolution is the change in inherited characteristics of a population over successive generations.
Populations, Not Individuals
Evolution happens at the population level. Individual organisms do not evolve — populations do.
Time Scale
Evolution can occur over millions of years, or within just a few generations (e.g., antibiotic resistance).
Key idea: Evolution is driven by heritable variation within populations. Without variation, there is nothing for selection to act on.
🐦
Darwin's Four Postulates
The foundation of natural selection
1
Variation
Individuals within a population show variation in their traits (e.g., beak size, fur color, disease resistance).
2
Heritability
Traits are passed from parents to offspring through genes. Only heritable traits can be shaped by natural selection.
3
Overproduction
Organisms produce more offspring than the environment can support, creating competition for limited resources.
4
Differential Survival & Reproduction
Individuals with favorable traits survive and reproduce more — "survival of the fittest." Their traits become more common over time.
🦋
Types of Natural Selection
How populations shift over time
Directional Selection
One extreme phenotype is favored. The population shifts over time. Example: antibiotic-resistant bacteria increasing as antibiotics are used.
Stabilizing Selection
Average phenotype is favored; extremes are selected against. Example: human birth weight — very small and very large babies have lower survival rates.
Disruptive Selection
Both extremes are favored over the intermediate phenotype. Can lead to speciation when two distinct groups form within a population.

Evidence for Evolution
Multiple independent lines of scientific support
Fossil Record
Fossils show a chronological sequence of organisms from ancient to modern forms, revealing transitional species.
Comparative Anatomy
Homologous structures (same structure, different function) reveal common ancestry. Example: human arm, whale flipper, bat wing.
Molecular Evidence
DNA and protein similarities across species show evolutionary relationships. Humans share ~98% of DNA with chimpanzees.
Observed Evolution
Antibiotic resistance in bacteria and the color shift of peppered moths during the industrial revolution are evolution observed in real time.
🌎
Speciation
How new species form from common ancestors
Geographic Isolation
Populations are physically separated by barriers (mountains, rivers, oceans). Genetic differences accumulate independently in each group.
Reproductive Isolation
Over time, isolated populations can no longer interbreed successfully — they become separate species. This is the definition of a new species.
Galapagos Finches — Classic Case Study
Darwin observed 13 finch species on the Galapagos, each with a beak adapted to a unique food source — all descended from one ancestor. This is adaptive radiation: rapid diversification from a single lineage into multiple ecological niches.

Key Vocabulary

Master these 12 terms — they appear on the quiz and on your Virginia SOL exam.

Evolution
Change in the heritable characteristics of a biological population over successive generations.
Natural Selection
The process by which organisms with favorable traits survive and reproduce more successfully than others, passing traits to offspring.
Adaptation
A heritable trait that increases an organism's fitness (survival and reproductive success) in its environment.
Fitness
An organism's ability to survive and reproduce in its environment relative to other individuals in the population.
Variation
Differences in traits among individuals in a population, arising from mutations and genetic recombination during reproduction.
Speciation
The evolutionary process by which populations evolve to become distinct species, usually through reproductive isolation.
Common Descent
The principle that all living things share a common ancestor, supported by DNA sequences and the fossil record.
Homologous Structures
Structures in different species that share the same evolutionary origin but may perform different functions (e.g., human arm and whale flipper).
Vestigial Structures
Body parts that have lost most of their ancestral function through evolution (e.g., human tailbone, whale pelvic bones).
Allele Frequency
How common a particular version of a gene is in a population. Natural selection changes allele frequencies over generations.
Fossil Record
The collection of all known fossils, providing a historical record of life on Earth and showing evolutionary change over time.
Adaptive Radiation
Rapid diversification of many species from a single ancestor into diverse ecological niches. Classic example: Darwin's Galapagos finches.
