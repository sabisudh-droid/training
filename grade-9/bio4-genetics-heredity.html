01 / VOCABULARY

Key Terms

Gene
A segment of DNA that codes for a specific trait or characteristic in an organism.
e.g., Gene for eye color
Allele
One of two or more versions of a gene. Each parent contributes one allele to offspring.
e.g., B (brown) or b (blue)
Genotype
The genetic makeup of an organism — the actual allele combination it carries.
e.g., BB, Bb, or bb
Phenotype
The physical expression of the genotype — the visible trait that results from the alleles.
e.g., brown eyes, tall plant
Dominant Allele
An allele that masks the expression of the recessive allele when present. Written in uppercase.
e.g., B (brown eye allele)
Recessive Allele
An allele whose expression is masked by the dominant allele. Expressed only when homozygous. Lowercase.
e.g., b (blue eye allele)
Homozygous
Having two identical alleles for a given trait. Can be homozygous dominant or recessive.
e.g., BB (dominant) or bb (recessive)
Heterozygous
Having two different alleles for a given trait. The dominant allele is expressed in phenotype.
e.g., Bb (shows dominant trait)
Punnett Square
A diagram used to predict the probability of offspring genotypes and phenotypes from a genetic cross.
Used for all trait crosses
Heredity
The passing of traits from parents to their offspring through genetic material (DNA).
e.g., Hair color from parents
Chromosome
A structure made of DNA and proteins that carries genes. Humans have 46 chromosomes (23 pairs).
23 pairs in human cells
Trait
A specific characteristic of an organism that can be observed and that can be inherited from parents.
e.g., height, ear shape

Gregor Mendel & Classical Inheritance

Father of Genetics

Austrian monk who lived 1822–1884
Conducted pea plant experiments in the 1850s–1860s
Studied 7 different traits over 8 years
Analyzed over 29,000 pea plants
Published findings in 1866 — widely ignored until the early 1900s
His work forms the foundation of modern genetics

Law I
Law of Segregation
Each organism has two alleles for each trait. During gamete formation, these alleles separate so each gamete carries only ONE allele. The alleles recombine randomly at fertilization.
Law II
Law of Independent Assortment
Alleles of different genes sort independently of one another during gamete formation. Inheriting one trait does not affect the inheritance of another trait.

04 / NON-MENDELIAN GENETICS

Beyond Simple Dominance

Not all traits follow Mendel's simple dominant/recessive rules. Several other patterns of inheritance exist that produce different ratios and phenotypes.

🌺 Incomplete Dominance

Neither allele is completely dominant. The heterozygous offspring shows a blend of both parent phenotypes.

Key Difference from Mendel: The phenotypic ratio matches the genotypic ratio — 3 distinct phenotypes instead of 2.
Examples: Snapdragons, four o'clock flowers, some hair textures
🩸 Codominance

Both alleles are fully expressed simultaneously in the heterozygote — no blending occurs. Both traits appear side-by-side.

Short Answer — Questions 6–10
6
What is the difference between genotype and phenotype? Give a specific example of each.
Model Answer: The genotype is the actual genetic makeup of an organism — the allele combination it carries (e.g., Bb). The phenotype is the physical, visible expression of that genotype (e.g., brown eyes). Two organisms can have different genotypes (BB and Bb) but the same phenotype (both show brown eyes).
7
A black (BB) cat mates with a white (bb) cat. List ALL possible genotypes and phenotypes of the offspring.
Model Answer: All offspring will be Bb (heterozygous). Since B (black) is dominant over b (white), all offspring will be black. Genotype: 100% Bb. Phenotype: 100% Black. This is a complete dominance cross.
8
Explain why AB blood type is an example of codominance and NOT incomplete dominance.
Model Answer: In type AB blood, both A and B antigens are fully expressed and present on red blood cells simultaneously — neither masks the other. This is codominance. Incomplete dominance would produce a brand new blended phenotype (like pink from red × white). AB blood does not produce a "blend" — it shows both A and B traits fully and separately at the same time.
9
A carrier woman (X^H X^h) and an unaffected man (X^H Y) have children. Draw a Punnett square and determine the probability their son will have color blindness.
Model Answer: Cross: X^H X^h × X^H Y → Offspring: X^H X^H (normal female), X^H X^h (carrier female), X^H Y (normal male), X^h Y (color blind male). Of the two possible sons, one is normal (X^H Y) and one has color blindness (X^h Y). Probability that a son has color blindness = 50%. Overall probability for any child = 25%.
10
How do polygenic traits differ from single-gene traits? Give two examples of polygenic traits in humans.
Model Answer: Single-gene traits produce a limited number of discrete phenotypes (e.g., 2 or 3). Polygenic traits are controlled by two or more genes and produce a continuous range of phenotypes — many possible outcomes distributed in a bell curve. They are also influenced by the environment. Two examples: (1) Human height — ranges from very short to very tall with all values in between; (2) Skin color — ranges from very light to very dark due to multiple pigment genes.
